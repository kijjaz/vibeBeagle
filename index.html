VTO OVERVIEW

Vibration Theory of Olfaction (VTO)

Conventional olfaction science suggests a "lock-and-key" shape model (odotope theory), where olfactory receptors recognize the geometric shapes of molecules. However, this model struggles to explain why molecules with identical shapes can smell completely different, or why molecules with totally different shapes (like sulfur compounds and boranes) can both smell strongly of rotten eggs.

The Vibration Theory of Olfaction, championed by biophysicist Luca Turin, proposes that olfactory receptors behave like miniature spectrometers. Receptors utilize Inelastic Electron Tunneling Spectroscopy (IETS) to measure the vibrational frequencies of chemical bonds (molecular vibrations) when an odorant is docked.

Specifically, when an odorant molecule docks inside the receptor's binding pocket, it acts as a bridge for an electron to tunnel across a potential gap. Tunneling is only possible (or highly amplified) when the energy of the electron matches a quantum of molecular vibration: $$eV = \hbar \omega = h \nu$$ where $V$ is the bias voltage across the receptor, $e$ is the charge of an electron, $\hbar$ is the reduced Planck constant, and $\omega = 2\pi\nu$ is the molecular normal mode frequency. This inelastic tunneling event triggers an intracellular G-protein cascade, sending a signal to the olfactory bulb.

Our Computational Simulation Pipeline

Since experimental infrared spectra are not available for every synthesized aroma chemical, vibeBeagle simulates the underlying vibrational dynamics using a physical model built in Python:

1. 3D Conformer Generation (RDKit)

The chemical structure is loaded from a SMILES string, hydrogens are added, and RDKit generates a stable 3D coordinates conformation of the molecule.

2. Geometry Optimization (UFF)

We use the Universal Force Field (UFF) to minimize the molecule's potential energy $V(\mathbf{R})$, relaxing the bond lengths and angles to their physical equilibrium state where forces $\mathbf{F}_i = -\nabla_i V = 0$.

3. Numerical Hessian & Normal Modes

The second derivatives of potential energy are computed numerically by displacing each atom's coordinates by a tiny step ($\delta = \pm 0.005$ Å) and measuring force gradient changes. The resulting mass-weighted Hessian matrix $H_{ij}$ is defined as: $$H_{ij} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{m_i m_j}} \frac{\partial^2 V}{\partial x_i \partial x_j}$$ Diagonalization of $H$ yields eigenvalues $\lambda_k = \omega_k^2$, which are converted to wavenumbers: $$\tilde{\nu}_k = \frac{\omega_k}{2\pi c} = \frac{1}{2\pi c} \sqrt{\lambda_k}$$

4. IR Intensity Estimation

Gasteiger partial charges $q_i$ are calculated for each atom. For each normal mode $k$ with displacements $\mathbf{u}_i^k$, the transition dipole derivative is computed by summing displacement-charge products: $$\frac{\partial \boldsymbol{\mu}}{\partial Q_k} = \sum_{i} q_i \mathbf{u}_i^k$$ The vibrational intensity $I_k$ is proportional to the square of this transition dipole derivative: $$I_k \propto \left\| \frac{\partial \boldsymbol{\mu}}{\partial Q_k} \right\|^2$$ Extremely symmetric modes cancel out and receive zero intensity, representing physical selection rules.

5. Gaussian Broadening & Grid Mapping

The discrete modes are broadened into a continuous curve using a Gaussian kernel with $\sigma = 25 \text{ cm}^{-1}$ over a 361-point grid from $400$ to $4000 \text{ cm}^{-1}$ (step $10 \text{ cm}^{-1}$): $$I(\tilde{\nu}) = \sum_{k} I_k \exp\left( -\frac{(\tilde{\nu} - \tilde{\nu}_k)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right)$$ The final curve is normalized to a peak height of 1.0.

Optimized Scent-Weighted Similarity Metric

Standard cosine similarity treats all wavenumbers with equal weight. However, organic molecules are dominated by aliphatic $\text{C-H}$ stretches around $2800\text{-}3000 \text{ cm}^{-1}$. This "structural scaffold noise" dilutes the presence of rare, odor-active functional groups in similarity calculations.

To resolve this, we optimized a **band-weighting profile** to maximize the clustering of known scent categories (e.g. musk, fruit, wood, spice) in coordinate space. For two vibration vectors $\vec{x}$ and $\vec{y}$, the similarity $S$ is calculated using the weighted cosine coefficient: $$S(\vec{x}, \vec{y}) = \frac{\sum_{i} w_i x_i y_i}{\sqrt{\sum_{i} w_i x_i^2} \sqrt{\sum_{i} w_i y_i^2}}$$ where $w_i$ is the weight of the band corresponding to frequency bin $i$:

Band Range (cm⁻¹) Weight Odor Relevance
Skeletal & Torsional Bends 400 - 700 1.2500 Low-frequency skeletal puckering, heavy backbones (musks, wood).
Alkene Out-of-Plane (OOP) 700 - 1000 1.3500 Stereochemical alkene geometry, crucial for green/fruity isomers.
C-O Stretches 1000 - 1300 1.6000 Key ester, lactone, and ether bonds (fruity, peach, floral characters).
Alkyl Methyl Deformation 1300 - 1400 0.6500 Symmetric methyl umbrella bending, down-weighted to suppress bulk alkyl noise.
Aromatic / Double Bonds 1400 - 1650 1.0231 Aromatic rings, conjugated double bonds.
Carbonyl (C=O) 1650 - 1800 1.5616 Key marker in esters (fruity), ketones, and aldehydes. Up-weighted by 56%.
Triple Bonds / Nitriles 2100 - 2260 15.0000 Nitriles (citrus/green). Very rare and highly potent; heavily prioritized.
Thiols (S-H) 2500 - 2600 0.1898 Sulfides/thiols. Distinctly sharp; down-weighted to prevent overriding.
Aliphatic C-H 2800 - 3000 0.3528 Generic organic paraffinic stretch. Down-weighted by 65% to ignore noise.
Aromatic C-H 3000 - 3150 0.7438 Benzene ring/alkenyl hydrogens.
Hydroxyl (O-H) 3150 - 3650 0.0425 Alcohols/phenols. Ignored to avoid hydrogen-bonding shifts and humidity noise.

Scent Bridge Finder Projection Mathematics

When two endpoints—Molecule A ($P_A$) and Molecule B ($P_B$)—are selected, we calculate a virtual transition line between them in the 2D MDS coordinate space:

1. Let $P_A = (x_A, y_A)$ and $P_B = (x_B, y_B)$ be the coordinates. We define the line segment vector from A to B as: $$\vec{v} = P_B - P_A = (x_B - x_A, y_B - y_A)$$ The squared length of the segment is: $$L^2 = \|\vec{v}\|^2 = (x_B - x_A)^2 + (y_B - y_A)^2$$

2. For any other compound $P_C = (x_C, y_C)$ in our database, we project it onto the line vector $\vec{v}$ to find its projection factor $t$ (representing progress along the path from A to B): $$t = \frac{(P_C - P_A) \cdot \vec{v}}{L^2} = \frac{(x_C - x_A)(x_B - x_A) + (y_C - y_A)(y_B - y_A)}{(x_B - x_A)^2 + (y_B - y_A)^2}$$

3. We filter for compounds where $0 < t < 1$, which places them physically between the two selected endpoints. The coordinates of the projected point on the line segment are: $$P_{\text{proj}} = P_A + t \vec{v} = (x_A + t v_x, y_A + t v_y)$$

4. We compute the perpendicular offset distance $d$ representing how close the candidate lies to the straight transition path: $$d = \|P_C - P_{\text{proj}}\| = \sqrt{(x_C - x_{\text{proj}})^2 + (y_C - y_{\text{proj}})^2}$$ Compounds with $d \le 120.0$ are classified as Bridge Candidates, showing perfumers the olfactory transition compounds that can "bridge" Molecule A and Molecule B.
